Reference · the evidence, documented in full

The Monte Carlo model, documented at every depth.

When this campaign says the ~$1.2M wireless upgrade is likely to pay for itself, that claim rests on a simulation — and a simulation you can't inspect is just a fancy way of saying “trust us.” This document is the inspection hatch. It explains the model three times, at three depths: once in plain English, once at the depth a board member or CFO works at, and once at the depth of the code itself — every distribution, every formula, every deliberate omission. I Layer 1 · Plain English

The whole model, no math required.

Six short sections. If you read nothing else on this page, read these — everything deeper down is just these six ideas with the engineering shown.

§ 1.1 · Layer 1

What this document is — and why it exists.

Okanogan County's PUD owns a rural wireless network. A ~$1.2 million upgrade to that network has been paused, and part of the debate is financial: would the upgrade pay for itself? This campaign built a financial model to answer that question, published the answer, and put a live calculator on the model page so anyone can test it.

But a model that only shows its answers asks for trust. This document removes the need for trust by showing the machinery: what the model assumes, where every number comes from, what it deliberately leaves out, and how to re-run it and get the same result to the decimal. It's written in three layers so that a neighbor, a commissioner, and an auditor can each read at the depth they need — and so that no one can honestly say the analysis is a black box.

Why three layers

A single write-up always fails someone: too technical and most readers bounce off; too simple and a skeptical expert calls it hand-waving. Layering solves both. Each layer makes the same claims — the deeper layers just carry more of the proof. If you ever find a place where Layer 1 says something Layer 3 doesn't back up, that's a bug in this document; tell us and we'll fix it.

§ 1.2 · Layer 1

The question the model answers.

On the surface, one question: if the District spends ~$1.2M upgrading the wireless network, does the money come back? But hiding inside it is a second question that matters just as much: who is the money supposed to come back to?

The upgrade pays two different parties. The District earns wholesale revenue from the local companies that resell service on its network. And county households save money, because a public, cost-based option keeps the private alternatives from charging captive-market prices. Those are different pockets — so the model keeps separate books for each, and answers both: does the District recoup its cost? and does the county as a whole come out ahead? (The second question turns out to be the sturdier one — §1.4 explains why.)

What the model is not

It is not a promise about the future. It is a disciplined way of asking: across the range of futures the evidence supports, how often does this investment work out? The output is odds, not prophecy — the same kind of claim as “a 70% chance of rain.”

§ 1.3 · Layer 1

A Monte Carlo, explained simply.

Nobody knows exactly how the next 15 years will go. Costs might run over. Inflation might bite. Starlink might poach more customers, or fewer. The usual spreadsheet handles that by picking one guess for each unknown and multiplying them out into one confident-looking answer — and hiding how much guessing just happened.

A Monte Carlo does the opposite. Instead of one guess per unknown, we give the computer a range for each — “the build probably costs about this, but could plausibly land anywhere between here and here” — with each range anchored to a document or record. Then the computer plays out the next 15 years 200,000 times. Each run rolls the dice within those ranges: in one future the build runs 20% over budget and inflation spikes; in another it comes in cheap and the customer base grows. Finally it just counts: in how many of those 200,000 futures did the upgrade pay for itself in time?

That count is the headline. In the central (“Balanced”) scenario, the District earns its money back in about 88% of simulated futures, and the county comes out ahead in ~100%. The method isn't exotic — the GAO, the Federal Transit Administration, and state DOTs require exactly this kind of analysis for capital projects — it's just rarely offered to the public with the hood open.

Worked example · one single run

Run #61,204 might look like: build cost lands at $1.31M (a bit over), inflation averages 2.8%, the margin draws 43%, the second-largest reseller exits in year 2 but half its customers move to other local resellers, Starlink churn draws 0.7%/yr. The model walks year 1 through year 15 with those cards dealt, and finds the balance hits zero in year 9.4 — a win, recorded as one tally mark. Then it deals fresh cards and does it again. 199,999 more times.

§ 1.4 · Layer 1

Two pockets, one county.

Here is the idea that makes this model different from a normal business case — and it's simpler than it sounds. The District charges local resellers a wholesale rate for the network. Every dollar of that rate is revenue to the District and a cost to the county's households (it flows through their bills). So when you add the District's books and the households' savings together honestly, the rate itself cancels out — like the same number appearing on both sides of a scale.

What's left after it cancels is the question that doesn't depend on rate policy at all: is it cheaper for the county to own this network than to leave its residents to a for-profit market with no public option? The noisy argument about what rate the District should charge only decides who keeps the surplus — the District's books or its ratepayers' pockets. It never decides whether the surplus exists.

That's why the model can show the District “losing” while the county wins: they're the same dollars in different pockets. And it's why the county-level answer is so stable across every scenario — it's immune to the one thing boards argue most about. The algebra behind the cancellation is three lines long: §3.9 the county math.

§ 1.5 · Layer 1

What the model found.

We ran it three ways — a skeptical auditor's world, a neutral analyst's, and a believer's (§2.3 defines them exactly). The honest summary:

88%
of Balanced-scenario futures: the District earns its ~$1.2M back within the gear's life

of Balanced futures: the county as a whole comes out ahead by year 10
8.9 yr

Read that last chip again, because it's the campaign's whole argument in one number: you can believe the gloomiest defensible story about the next 15 years — Starlink keeps poaching, a reseller collapses, the rate stays frozen — and the county still comes out ahead about 88% of the time at ten years. The scenarios disagree about how fast the District's own books recover (from 19% in the harshest accounting to 97% in the favorable one). They barely disagree about the county.

The honest caveat, up front

A model is only as good as its inputs, and some of ours are labeled judgments, not documents (every one is tagged, and the two biggest gaps — a firm tower-labor estimate and the wireless-only cost split — are named in §2.9). What we can promise is narrower and checkable: every input is published with its basis, the biggest ones are anchored to audited records, the judgment calls lean against the campaign's conclusion, and the whole thing re-runs to identical numbers on anyone's computer.

§ 1.6 · Layer 1

How you can check it — three ways, easiest first.

  1. Drag the sliders. The live calculator runs ~8,000 futures in your browser. Don't like an assumption? Change it and watch both answers move. Nothing to install, nothing to trust.
  2. Read the inputs. Every variable, its range, and its source — marked DOCUMENTED (from the District's own workbook, audit, or billing records) or INFERRED (a labeled modeling judgment) — is in the registers at §3.12 §3.13. If you want to challenge the model, that's where its load-bearing walls are.
  3. Re-run the code. The full model is a single public Python file, sources/analysis/montecarlo_v2.py, with its random seed locked and published. python montecarlo_v2.py reproduces every figure on the model page exactly — see §3.14 reproducibility.

That third option is the real answer to “why should I trust advocacy math?” You shouldn't have to. A model you can re-run, inspect, and break in public is the opposite of a rigged one — and the record of what our own audits corrected is published too (§3.15 audit history).

II Layer 2 · Working knowledge

The briefing for people who sign things.

Everything a commissioner, finance officer, or reporter needs to interrogate the model competently — the structure, the sourcing, the sensitivities — without reading a line of code.

§ 2.1 · Layer 2

The model at a glance.

The model is a 15-year annual cash simulation of the wireless line, repeated 200,000 times with the uncertain inputs drawn fresh each run. Structurally it's three stages:

1 · Draw a future

Each uncertain input — build cost, margin, inflation, gear life, churn, growth, the reseller exit — is drawn from an evidence-anchored range. Correlated risks are drawn together, not independently (§3.7).

→

2 · Walk 15 years

Year by year: subscribers evolve, revenue and operating cost grow at their drawn rates, cash is discounted, growth equipment is charged, events (exit, shock, overbuild) land in their drawn years.

→

3 · Tally

Did the discounted balance reach zero within the deadline — and when? Did the county end up ahead? Percentages and P50/P80 percentiles are read off the pile.

Two design commitments distinguish it from a typical pro forma: uncertainty is explicit (ranges and odds, never single guesses), and the beneficiary is explicit (separate ledgers for the District and for households, never blended — §2.2).

Scale & scope

Horizon 15 years; 200,000 iterations per configuration; all cash discounted at a Fisher nominal rate (~4% at base inflation); results reported with a standard error of ±0.11 points.

§ 2.2 · Layer 2

Five questions, five ledgers.

The code answers five distinct questions, kept strictly separate — results from different ledgers are never summed, because they answer different payees' questions. Lettered as in the source:

LedgerQuestionHeadline result
A · Cost recoveryDoes the wireless line's own discounted cash earn the ~$1.2M back within the equipment's life?Balanced: 88.4%, median payback 8.9 yr — §3.8
B · Ratepayer benefitHow much do households avoid overpaying because the cost-based public rate disciplines the market?P50 $463K (10 yr) / $921K (15 yr) — §3.9
C · County valueCounting both A and B honestly (the rate cancels): does the county come out ahead?Balanced: 99.8% at 10 yr — §3.9
D · ScenariosHow do A and C look under three coherent worldviews (pessimistic / balanced / optimistic)?The table in §2.3
E · Build vs run-to-failureHow does building now compare against spending nothing and letting the end-of-life network decline?Protected cash P50 ≈ $1.28M — §3.10

Ledger A is the deliberately hard, self-contained test — the number a skeptical auditor asks for. Ledger C is the decision-relevant one for the county. Ledger E reframes the whole question: Ledger A measures the upgrade against a frozen network that keeps every customer, but the District's own engineers describe the current gear as end-of-life and congested, so the true alternative is decline — which makes A's framing itself conservative.

Why never summed

Ledger A pays the District; Ledger B pays households. Summing them would double-count the wholesale rate (it's revenue in one and savings in the other). Ledger C is the only place they're combined, and it does so by an identity in which the rate cancels — that's what makes the combination honest. See the three-line derivation in §3.9.

§ 2.3 · Layer 2

The three scenarios — coherent worldviews, not dial-twiddling.

Single sensitivity toggles miss how the world actually varies: bad things travel together, and policy choices move several quantities at once. So the headline results come from three complete worldviews, each moving rate policy, accounting method, competitive stress, and retention credits together (exact parameter sets in §3.11):

ScenarioIn one sentenceDistrict recoupsCounty ahead 10→15 yrMin-year DSCR
PessimisticRate frozen forever, strictest recover-before-wear-out accounting, Starlink surge and a market shock, only a token 6% for-profit premium. 19.3% 87.6% → 98.2% 0.37×
Balanced leadOrdinary decade: partial rate recovery (60%), full-life accounting with a paid refresh, evidence-anchored retention and growth toward the District's own funded radio plan, 12% premium. 88.4% 99.8% → 100.0% 1.23×
OptimisticThe utility simply rates the way utilities normally do (full cost recovery), coverage gains land, 22% premium — the favorable end of defensible, not fantasy. 96.9% 100.0% → 100.0% 1.35×

Two things to notice. First, the District column swings 5× across worldviews while the county column barely moves — that's the rate-cancellation at work (§1.4). Second, the pessimistic District figure (19.3%) is dominated by choices, not fears: freeze the rate and demand recovery before physical wear-out, and even a healthy line looks bad on paper. The same pessimistic world measured with ordinary utility accounting recovers 32.0%; crediting the documented bandwidth line lifts it to 39.7%.

No probabilities on worldviews

We deliberately put no likelihood on which scenario “happens.” Any such number would be an invented prior dressed as analysis — and the argument doesn't need one, because the county-level conclusion holds in all three.

§ 2.4 · Layer 2

Where the inputs come from.

Every input carries one of two tags, and the distinction is enforced all the way into the variable registers (§3.12 §3.13):

  • DOCUMENTED — taken from a specific record: the District's own Tarana upgrade workbook (hardware counts and prices), the Washington State Auditor's 2024 audited segment results (the margin anchor), the District's budgets and RSP billing records obtained by public-records request (the bandwidth line, tower leases, transit cost), or a published standard (AACE 18R-97 cost-contingency classes).
  • INFERRED — a modeling judgment we own: labeled, bounded wide, and set leaning against our conclusion where the record is silent (tower-side labor hours, Starlink churn range, inflation regime weights).

§ 2.5 · Layer 2

What moves the answer — and what barely does.

Sensitivity runs from the Balanced base (~88% District payback), one lever at a time, full 200,000-run model. The pattern worth internalizing: the biggest movers are the board's own choices, not the market's threats.

LeverKindDistrict paybackshift

Three readings. (1) No single market fear sinks the case below a coin flip, and even all three landing together holds ~66% — while their joint probability is well under 1% (they're individually unlikely and largely independent). (2) The rate-policy lever (−24) moves the District's number more than any threat — and moves the county's number almost not at all, which is the cancellation again. (3) The delay lever is the one the board fully controls: each year of waiting costs roughly 3–4 points of payback probability (§3.6 for the mechanism).

§ 2.6 · Layer 2

The conservatism register — every thumb we put on the scale points down.

Where a judgment call was required, we consistently chose the direction that hurts the campaign's conclusion. The full list, so a reviewer can audit the claim rather than take it:

  • Growth mode is zero. The subscriber record shows a flat-to-declining base, so baseline organic growth is centered at 0% (range −6% to +5%) — no optimism about demand, despite ~9% of county homes lacking internet.
  • Bandwidth is credited at ~1/3 of documented. The books imply ~2.8 Mbps/customer of billed 95th-percentile usage; the headline credits 1.0 — specifically to absorb any chance the audited margin already contains part of that line (§3.4).
  • The counterfactual is wholesale, not retail. Household savings are measured against an escalating wholesale rate, not retail Starlink at ~$120/mo — understating what captive households would actually pay.
  • The floor scenario freezes the rate for 15 years while costs inflate (the “margin scissors”) — harsher than the District's own record of periodic adjustments elsewhere.
  • Sales tax is charged in full (8%) even though an exemption may apply; terminal value is $0 (towers, leases, and the customer base persisting past year 15 are counted as worthless); the $40 tier takes an elasticity haircut (2.5% fewer takers).
  • The macro factor's stress side is fully loaded — a bad decade raises churn, drags growth, and raises overbuild odds simultaneously (§3.7).
  • The NCI exit is treated as near-certain (85%) and timed realistically (year 2), which strands the already-bought radios instead of crediting a capital saving — adopted precisely because it lowered our headline (§3.15).
  • The browser calculator reads a few points lower than the full model (triangular draws, 8K runs) — so the number a visitor checks is the conservative one (§3.1).
§ 2.7 · Layer 2

Deliberate exclusions — with reasons.

A rigorous model is judged as much by what it declines to model. Each exclusion below was considered and rejected for a stated reason — mostly because modeling it would manufacture precision the evidence can't support:

ExcludedReason
Population declineThe county has grown every decade since 1990. Assuming a sustained decline would invent a headwind the record contradicts. (Households also form even when population is flat.)
Financing / interest costThe capex is reserve-funded by a cost-based utility, not borrowed at a return; modeled at $0 and disclosed. (Debt-service coverage is still reported as a stress metric — §2.8.)
Copulas / extreme-value machineryThe loss is bounded by the capex; a shared latent factor captures the dependence that matters. Heavier machinery adds opacity, not insight.
Regime-switching price modelsThose describe commodity markets. This rate is cost-based and District-set; it doesn't follow a stochastic market price.
Spectrum interference as a probabilityCo-location already works on these sites; a numeric interference risk would be invented. Handled as a policy note.
BEAD / statutory policy as a smooth distributionDeeply uncertain and two-sided; handled as discrete stress framing rather than a fake bell curve.
Terminal / going-concern valueSet to $0. Counting it would only raise the result; leaving it out is free conservatism.
A separate obsolescence hazardRemoved in v2: the random 8/11/14-year effective life already blends physical and economic aging; stacking a second hazard on top would double-count technology risk (§3.15).
§ 2.8 · Layer 2

Reading the outputs like a finance officer.

Percentiles, not averages

Every quantity is reported as a distribution: P50 for the typical case, P80 and P90 for the planning-grade tail. The build is funded at the P80 cost ($1.46M in the scenario configuration), not the bare hardware sum — that's what “contingency included” means concretely.

Conditional vs unconditional payback — don't mix them

“Median payback 8.9 years” is conditional — the typical timing among futures that recover. The unconditional read counts every future, including the ones that never pay back: in the Balanced case the unconditional P50 is 9.3 years and the P90 is “not reached”. Both are published (§3.8); quoting only the conditional one without saying so is how models get accused of spin.

DSCR — three different coverage numbers, labeled

DSCR is reported three ways, because they answer different questions: year-1 coverage (the first covenant test, 1.45× Balanced), minimum-year coverage (the worst single year over the term — the number a bond analyst actually reads: 1.23× Balanced), and average coverage (1.4×, the loosest read — an earlier version mislabeled this one as “DSCR” and it was corrected, §3.15). Debt is sized on the funded P80 capex at 4% over 15 years ($132K/yr in the scenario runs) — conservative against the District's actual ~2.9% 2020 bond cost. And context that belongs next to any sub-1.0× scenario figure: the whole telecom segment's audited surplus covers that same debt several times over regardless.

A “miss” is not money vanished

When a run fails the payback test, the median discounted shortfall is $143K (Balanced) — on a ~$1.2M spend, with the towers, the 200-mile backbone, and the customer relationships still standing. The failure mode is “the first generation of electronics didn't fully self-amortize,” not “the money is gone.”

§ 2.9 · Layer 2

What the model cannot tell you.

  • Which scenario will happen. It quantifies each worldview; it assigns no probability across them, on purpose (§2.3).
  • The two unpublished numbers. Tower-side labor and the wireless-only opex split are ranges until the District publishes them (§2.4). If the true values land outside our ranges, the results move — that's what the sliders are for.
  • Anything about service quality, coverage equity, or resilience. This is a financial model only; those arguments live on their own pages with their own evidence (coverage, resilience, equity).
  • Precision beyond its inputs. The claim is never “exactly 88.4%”; it's “in the central case, recovery is much more likely than not, and the county comes out ahead almost regardless.” The rigor is in the transparency and the ranges, not the decimal place.
  • Whether the board should build. The model prices the choice; weighing bounded, recoverable financial risk against an irreversible loss of the asset is the decision itself — argued at Weighing the Risk, not here.

Why two P80 capex figures appear in the outputs

The base cost-recovery runs report a funded P80 of $1.38M, while the three scenarios report $1.46M. Both are correct: the scenarios time the NCI exit at year 2 (nci_year = 2), which means the radios for its customers are purchased and later stranded — no capital saving — whereas the base configuration models an at-start exit whose unbought radios reduce capex. The year-2 timing was adopted because measuring both showed the at-start default flattered the headline (§3.15). Debt service follows the same logic: $124,406/yr on the base P80, $132K/yr on the scenario P80.

Version history is embedded in the source header: v2.1-2026-07-03 is the promoted v2.1 build (2026-07-03), successor to recovery_model.py — which randomized only margin and growth against a fixed capex, the exact weakness a board member's “where's the labor and contingency?” question exposed. v2's changes are catalogued in §3.15.

§ 3.2 · Layer 3

The distribution toolkit.

Five kinds of randomness cover every input. Each was chosen for a reason, and the choice is part of the model's defensibility:

PERT — for bounded expert estimates

Anything with a hard floor and ceiling and a most-likely value — costs, margins, gear life — is drawn PERT: a Beta distribution shaped by (min, mode, max) with the standard λ = 4 weighting.

pert(a, b, c): # min a, mode b, max c α = 1 + 4(b−a)/(c−a) β = 1 + 4(c−b)/(c−a) draw = a + Beta(α, β) × (c−a)
Why not clipped normal curves

A Normal clipped to a range piles probability mass at the clip points — the model would keep drawing “exactly the worst case,” which is an artifact, not a belief. PERT respects the bounds smoothly and is the three-point standard in GAO/PMI cost practice. (The browser calculator substitutes triangular draws — same three anchor points, simpler shape, slightly fatter shoulders — which is one reason it reads lower; §3.1.)

Bernoulli events — for things that either happen or don't

The NCI exit (p = 0.85), a fiber overbuild (p ≈ 0.12 over the decade, macro-adjusted), a market shock (p = 0.20 when the scenario arms it): each run flips the weighted coin once, and if the event lands it gets a drawn year and a drawn severity too. This is how “NCI leaves” can be near-certain without every run pretending it happens on day one.

Mixture — for inflation's two moods

Inflation isn't one bell curve; decades come in regimes. Each run draws its regime first — 88% chance of a normal decade (Normal, mean 2.5%, sd 1.0%), 12% chance of a high-inflation decade (mean 5.5%, sd 1.2%) — then clips to [0%, 9%]. The weights are a labeled judgment on BLS CPI history including the 2021–23 episode.

Uniform — for genuinely flat ignorance

Where the evidence gives bounds but no interior shape (the opex pass-through β in [1.0, 1.2], baseline Starlink churn in [0.3%, 1.2%]), the draw is uniform — claiming a mode would be pretending to know more than we do.

A shared latent factor — for correlation

One standard-normal draw Z per run feeds several inputs at once, so adverse conditions arrive together instead of averaging out. Full loading map in §3.7.

§ 3.3 · Layer 3

Capital cost build-up — from bill of materials to funded budget.

The capex is assembled per run, component by component:

# documented base (District Tarana workbook) hardware_mode = 370,783 (7-site tower hardware) + 878 × $787 (radio: RN $640 + BW unlock $100 + 1yr mgmt $47) = $1,061,769 hardware ~ PERT(−6%, mode, +6%) # spread = the $455-vs-$640 radio price sheet range labor ~ PERT($50K, $95K, $200K) # the BOM carries $0 tower-side labor — modeled, not ignored overrun ~ PERT(−10%, +5%, +40%) # AACE 18R-97 Class-4 planning-grade contingency capex = (hardware × 1.08 + labor) × (1 + overrun) # 1.08 = WA sales/use tax

Resulting distribution (base configuration): P50 $1.27M, P80 $1.38M = the funded budget, P90 $1.44M — against the bare $1.06M hardware sticker. Three further capital charges land inside the simulation years:

  • Growth radios. Net subscriber additions are charged their radio cost in the year they arrive, with electronics deflation of 4%/yr (radio_t = 787 × 0.96t−1). Growth is never free.
  • The G2 refresh. Under full-life accounting, one base-node refresh ~ PERT($110K, $150K, $210K) is charged at the drawn wear-out year. (Customer radios are a reimbursed pass-through — the District fronts them and RSPs/customers pay them back — so the refresh charges only District-owned gear; we still carry the full initial radio fleet in capex and credit no reimbursement, which is one-sided in the skeptic's favor.)
  • Shock rebuild. If the scenario arms a market shock and it lands, a rebuild cost scaled at $200K per 25% of subscriber loss is charged in the shock year.
Worked example · why the “$1.2M question” is really a $1.38M budget

Take the mode of everything: $1,061,769 hardware × 1.08 tax = $1,146,711; + $95K labor = $1,241,711; × 1.05 mode overrun ≈ $1.30M as the typical all-in cost. Funding at P80 instead of the mode adds the contingency a planning-grade estimate demands — $1.38M. The public shorthand “~$1.2M” sits between the sticker and the funded figure; the model never uses the shorthand.

§ 3.4 · Layer 3

The revenue stack — two billed lines, one lever each.

Line 1 · wholesale access

Base ARPU is $480/yr (the proposed $40/mo single tier; the currently-realized blended ~$31.2/mo = $374.40/yr is run as an explicit stress). At the $40 step the take-rate gets a 2.5% elasticity haircut. The rate then grows at g_arpu, which is where rate policy enters:

g_arpu = RT × g_opex + (1 − RT) × βarpuπ # RT = rate-tracking policy lever, 0..1 βarpu ~ U(0.3, 0.6) # a lagging cost-based rate recovers only part of inflation RT = 0 (frozen, the 17-yr record) · 0.6 (Balanced) · 1.0 (full cost recovery)

Line 2 · wholesale bandwidth resale

A separate, currently-billed line on every RSP invoice ($193K in 2025, distinct from the $1.14M access line; billed on the 95th percentile at $1.50/Mbps, with upstream transit cost ~$0 since the flat-rate 100G NoaNet link). Per customer per year:

bw_ps = M × $1.50 × 12 × 0.90 × 1.05t−1 M ~ PERT(0.5, 1.0, 2.0) Mbps # headline mode 1.0 — the books imply ~2.8 0.90 = margin allowance · 1.05 = net usage growth/yr
Why credit only a third of the documented figure

Two honest reasons: a residual chance the audited segment margin already reflects part of this line (we can't prove a clean split without an access-only cost allocation), and modesty. The scenarios therefore publish an explicit overlap bracket: assume the margin already holds all of it (add nothing — Balanced falls to 76.4%), half (90.9%), or none (add the documented ~2.8 — 97.2%). The headline 1.0 Mbps sits near the ~64%-overlap point — well into the cautious half of the bracket. Meanwhile the pressure runs the other way: these 878 sit on the congested towers where usage is being suppressed, and per-customer demand has risen every year for two decades.

Demand-pull — the flat rate's volume dividend

A rate held below inflation gets more competitive every year, which pulls price-sensitive customers in. The model converts the widening gap into take-up with a necessity-grade elasticity:

pull = 0.30 × max((π + 1.2%) − g_arpu, 0) # big when the rate is frozen; ~0 at full recovery

A sensitivity run with pull forced to zero isolates its contribution: Balanced 88.4% → 86.0% — real but modest, about 2 points.

§ 3.5 · Layer 3

Operating cost & margin — the scissors, the bathtub, the anchor.

The single most load-bearing input is the cash margin, and it is deliberately not a number we chose: the center is the WA State Auditor's 2024 telecom segment result (~45–50% cash), widened to PERT(28%, 45%, 58%) because the wireless-vs-fiber split within the segment is unpublished. Opex is then anchored in dollars, not as a percentage of whatever the price happens to be:

opex₀ = (1 − margin) × $480 + $12 # dollar cost-to-serve at the $40 reference — identical for the $31 run opex_t = opex₀ × (1 + g_opex)t × bathtub_t g_opex = βopexπ, βopex ~ U(1.0, 1.2) # costs track or outrun inflation bathtub_t = 1 + 0.02 × max(t − 8, 0) # aging-gear failure/RMA ramp in the final third
The “margin scissors,” mechanically

Because βopex ≥ 1.0 while a frozen or lagging rate grows at βarpuπ < π, margin is derived fresh each year and erodes whenever the rate under-tracks costs. A run can never pair flat costs with a rising rate — the coupling through the same drawn π forbids the cheat. This single mechanism is most of why the frozen-rate floor (RT 0) scores 44.9% while full recovery scores 79.4%.

Scenario stress: an armed price war adds F4 × revenue to opex for the first three years (default 2 margin points). The June-22 board question — “does that include any O&M?” — is answered structurally here: every cash figure in every ledger is net of this opex line; there is no gross-revenue path through the model.

§ 3.6 · Layer 3

Subscriber dynamics — growth, churn, events, and the cap.

The subscriber path is where most of the model's moving parts meet. Starting base: 878 served customers (net of the NCI adjustment below and, at the $40 tier, the elasticity haircut). Each year:

subs_t = min( subs_{t−1} × (1 + growth + pull) × (1 − churn_t), cap ) growth ~ PERT(−6%, 0% + U3, +5% + U3) − 0.004Z # mode 0: the record shows a flat-to-shrinking base churn_t = churn₀ × (1 + 0.08 × min(t−1, 6)) + 0.0015Z, churn₀ ~ U(0.3%, 1.2%) cap = 1,035 funded radios (+ U1 coverage upside when armed)
  • Churn is front-loaded and plateaus. The 8%/yr ramp on the Starlink hazard stops growing at year 7 — LEO capacity saturates and its prices have risen, so a forever-compounding hazard would contradict the physics and the record. An armed Starlink surge adds U(0.75, 1.25) × F1 on top; the local-stickiness credit subtracts U2, floored at zero.
  • Growth is capped at funded capacity. No run can invent customers beyond the 1,035 radios the District's own plan funds (plus documented coverage upside when the scenario arms it). Demand headroom exists — the cap binds anyway.
  • The NCI exit (Bernoulli p = 0.85) removes only the net loss: NCI holds ~22% of the served base (second-largest reseller — Highland is larger), and about half its book migrates to other local resellers on the same network, so the net step is ~11%. Timing semantics matter and are documented in §3.1: at-start exits save the unbought radios; the adopted year-2 timing strands them. Its wholesale-bandwidth purchases for own-tower customers continue either way — and are not credited, another one-sided rounding against us.
  • Build delay (the calculator's waiting lever): each delayed year multiplies the starting base by (1 − 0.04 × years), floored at 50% — the congested network sheds customers to Starlink while it waits, and the longer they're gone the harder the win-back. This is the ~3–4 points/yr in §2.5.
  • Overbuild and market shock land as one-time steps in their drawn years: overbuild (p ≈ 0.12, macro-adjusted; year U{3..10}) cuts 8–18% of a site; an armed shock (p = 0.20, year U{3..7}) cuts F3 and charges the rebuild (§3.3).
A step, not a compounding slide

v2 fixed a real bug here: the overbuild loss was originally applied every year from the event year (t >= overbuild_yr), silently compounding an 8–18% one-time loss into a runaway decline. The corrected code applies it once (t == overbuild_yr) as a persistent level shift. The bug made results worse than intended; fixing it is part of the audit trail (§3.15).

§ 3.7 · Layer 3

Macro & dependence — inflation, the discount, and the shared factor.

One inflation, many consequences

Each run draws a single inflation rate π from the two-regime mixture (§3.2) and feeds it everywhere at once: operating costs grow at βopexπ; a lagging rate grows at βarpuπ; the discount rate is the Fisher nominal 1.5% + π; and the for-profit counterfactual escalates at βcomπ + real creep with βcom ~ U(1.0, 1.2) (§3.9). One draw, consistent consequences — a high-inflation run hurts the District's margin and widens households' savings gap, both of which are true at once in the real world.

The shared macro factor Z

Z ~ N(0,1) + 0.4 × 1high-inflation regime loadings: churn += 0.0015 Z growth −= 0.004 Z P(overbuild) = clip(0.12 + 0.03 Z, 0, 1)

Z is the “kind of decade” dial. A bad decade (Z high) brings more churn, weaker growth, and higher overbuild odds simultaneously — the correlated stress a pro forma can't represent. The +0.4 shift ties macro stress to the high-inflation regime. As of the 2026-06 de-bias audit Z is symmetric: a benign decade genuinely helps, instead of the earlier adverse-only loading (which was double-conservative; the correction is logged in §3.15).

Why a latent factor instead of a correlation matrix

With three correlated quantities and bounded marginals, a single latent loading is transparent, sufficient, and auditable at a glance. A full rank-correlation or copula apparatus would model dependence we have no data to calibrate — the same false-precision trap catalogued in §2.7.

Discounting

All cash in every ledger is discounted at disc = 0.015 + π per run (~4% nominal at base). Coupling the discount to the run's own inflation prevents the classic error of discounting a high-inflation future at a negative real rate. Nominal cash, nominal discount, consistently — and the choice of a 1.5% real rate is conservative against a public utility's actual cost of funds (its 2020 bond carried ~2.9% nominal).

§ 3.8 · Layer 3

Ledger A — the payback math, line by line.

The core recursion tracks a discounted unrecovered balance, starting at the drawn capex and paid down (or up) each year:

bal₀ = capex cash_t = subs_t × (rev_t − opex_t + bw_t) # may be NEGATIVE — no floor bal_t = bal_{t−1} − cash_t/df_t + netAdd_t × radio_t/df_t # df_t = (1+disc)^t (+ g2_cost/df at the drawn wear-out year, full-life mode) (+ rebuild/df at the shock year, if armed) payback: first t with bal_t ≤ 0 and t ≤ deadline, interpolated sub-year: pyr = (t−1) + bal_{t−1}/(bal_{t−1} − bal_t)

The deadline — two accounting conventions, both published

  • Strict cliff (cliff=True, the Pessimistic convention): the balance must clear before the gear physically wears out — a PERT(8, 11, 14)-year drawn life. No refresh is charged, because recovery past the cliff doesn't count anyway. The harshest defensible test.
  • Full-life (cliff=False, Balanced/Optimistic): recovery is measured over the 15-year horizon, and in exchange the model honestly charges the G2 refresh at wear-out. This is how utilities actually recover capital — through successive refresh cycles, each paid for by the revenue it enables.

The convention alone is worth ~17 points in the Balanced world (§2.5) — which is why the model publishes each scenario under both conventions (district_alt_method in the outputs) rather than letting the choice hide inside a headline.

§ 3.9 · Layer 3

Ledgers B & C — the county math, and the cancellation proved.

Ledger B · ratepayer surplus avoided

What households don't overpay because the cost-based public rate exists, discounted over the served base:

PV_B = Σt subs_t × (P_com,t − P_pub,t) / (1+disc)t P_pub,t = $480 × 1.015t # flat cost-based drift P_com,t = $480 × Π(1+g₂), g₂ = clip(βcomπ + creep + noise, 0, 11%) βcom ~ U(1.0,1.2), creep ~ N(1.2%, 1%) # a for-profit passes inflation through and creeps above it

Results: 10-year P50 $463K (P5 $14K – P95 $1,086K); 15-year P50 $921K. The headline applies the near-certain NCI exit to the base; the no-exit and full-22%-loss runs bracket it ($795K – $1,013K at 15 years). Escalation is floored at zero — conceding per-megabit price deflation — and the counterfactual is wholesale, not retail Starlink. Never summed with Ledger A.

Ledger C · the identity that makes the rate cancel

county = households + District = subs(P_com − P_pub) + [ subs(P_pub − opex + bw) − capex ] = −capex + subs(P_com − opex + bw) # P_pub cancels — line three is the proof

The public rate appears with opposite signs in the two terms and vanishes from the sum. What remains — what a commercial provider would extract, minus the real cost to serve, minus the build — is independent of rate policy. Implementation subtleties that make the invariance genuine rather than approximate:

  • The demand-pull term inside subs uses the flat-rate gap (gAb) regardless of RT, so the subscriber path itself doesn't secretly move with rate policy.
  • Growth-radio capex and shock rebuilds are charged inside the county flow too — the county pays real costs, not just enjoys transfers.
  • The for-profit premium lever (PREM) starts the counterfactual above cost: 0% is the equal-anchor floor (conceding a for-profit would price exactly at our cost); 12–22% are the scenario settings; observed gaps run 30–50%.

At the floor — premium 0, every concession granted — the county is ahead in 92.0% of 10-year futures (P50 $478K) and 99.3% at 15. With the modest 15% premium the record supports, those become 99.8% and 100.0%. And the decomposition shows the two-pockets story explicitly: in 57.5% of floor runs the District's own books lose — yet in 49.6% (of all runs) the county is ahead anyway. Same dollars, different pockets.

The scenario that can lose — on purpose

A result that can't fail is a definition, not a finding. So Ledger C has a falsifier switch: competitive=True sets P_com = P_pub — a market so competitive there's no overpayment to avoid — and the county advantage collapses to the District's own payback (pessimistic 15-yr county-ahead falls from 98.2% to 47.9%). The county conclusion therefore depends on rural Okanogan not being a competitively-priced market — which is exactly the condition the public network exists to address, and the reason the result is a finding.

§ 3.10 · Layer 3

Ledger E — build now vs run-to-failure.

Ledger A deliberately measures the upgrade against a frozen network that keeps every customer forever. That's the cleanest self-contained test — and it flatters the do-nothing option, because the actual do-nothing path is an end-of-life, congested network the District's own engineers say cannot expand. Ledger E prices the real comparison: build now, or spend nothing and run the old gear until it retires or fails.

Method — shared futures. Both paths are evaluated inside the same drawn future (identical inflation, discount, margin, bandwidth draws), so the difference is the value of the capital decision, not two different worlds. The build path reuses Ledger A's conservative growth engine and pays its growth radios plus a pro-rated G2 refresh. The run-to-failure path keeps earning — but declines at a worsening rate, pays an emergency-maintenance premium on dying gear, and hard-retires at a drawn remaining-life year:

RTF path: decline_t = d₀ × (1 + decay × (t−1)); subs_r → 0 at fail_yr ~ PERT(1, mode, hi) opex_r = opex_t × (1 + ramp × t) # used-parts / emergency premium incremental NPV = −capex + Σ (cash_build − cash_rtf)/df protected cash = Σ (cash_build − cash_rtf)/df # gross, no capex

The decline rate is bracketed, not asserted. Three transparent cases — the destination (eventual zero at hardware retirement) is the near-certain part; the speed is the scenario:

Read this table honestly — both directions. The build protects $0.99M–$1.46M of present-value cash across the bracket — on the order of the capex itself, which is the basis for the published “avoided decline” framing. At the same time, the pure 15-year incremental NPV is negative at the median in the slower-decline cases. Why both are true: run-to-failure spends nothing and still harvests cash while it bleeds, and the window slams shut at year 15 — every customer, tower, and revenue stream the build preserves beyond year 15 is counted at zero (the same $0 terminal value as everywhere else, §2.7), while run-to-failure's path conveniently ends at zero with nothing left to truncate. The window asymmetry runs entirely against the build. That's why this ledger is published as a bracket with its mechanics showing, is never blended into Ledger A's headline — and why the honest one-line summary is about the protected cash and the near-certain shutdown (100% of central-case run-to-failure paths are dark by year 10), not a cherry-picked NPV from either end.

Inferred, and labeled

The seasonal leave-and-return erosion (recov_decay) is the one place a texture from the record enters as a parameter we can't document precisely — so it's exposed as a scenario input, bracketed, and never asserted as fact. NCI is common-mode here: the same exit hits both paths identically, so it cancels from the comparison by construction.

§ 3.14 · Layer 3

Reproducibility & convergence.

  • Locked seed. All randomness flows from NumPy's default_rng(20260629) (offset seeds for the C and E engines so ledgers don't share draws they shouldn't). Anyone running python montecarlo_v2.py gets every figure on this page identically — there is no “lucky run” to fish for.
  • Convergence. At n = 200,000, the binomial standard error on a headline percentage is √(p(1−p)/n) ≈ ±0.11 points — the Monte Carlo noise is two orders of magnitude smaller than the input uncertainty it exists to measure. (The browser calculator's 8,000 runs wobble visibly — ±0.5pt — which is why the published headlines come from the full model.)
  • Version pinning. The output JSONs embed model_version (v2.1-2026-07-03) and the seed, so a figure can always be traced to the exact code that produced it. This documentation is written against that version; the pages read the JSONs live.
  • Test harness. test_v2.py ships alongside the model — invariance checks (the county total's rate-independence among them) run against the same seed.
Re-run it yourself

cd sources/analysis && python montecarlo_v2.py — NumPy is the only dependency. Runtime is a few minutes; it prints each ledger's summary and rewrites the five output JSONs. Diff them against the committed copies: they should be byte-identical.

§ 3.17 · Layer 3

Glossary.

Every dotted-underlined term on this page, in one place. Deep-linkable: each entry has its own anchor.

Monte Carlo simulation
A method that runs a calculation thousands of times, drawing each uncertain input at random from an evidence-based range, then reads odds and percentiles off the pile of results. Named for the casino; standard practice in federal capital budgeting.
Iteration / run
One complete simulated 15-year future with one set of drawn inputs. This model runs 200,000 per configuration.
Distribution
The shape of an uncertain input: which values are possible and how likely each is. The honest replacement for a single guess.
PERT
A smooth three-point (min / most-likely / max) distribution built on a Beta curve, standard for expert-estimated quantities. Respects hard bounds without piling probability at them the way a clipped Normal does. §3.2 has the formula.
Bernoulli event
A weighted coin flip: the event happens in this run or it doesn't, with probability p. Used for the NCI exit, overbuild, and market shock.
Mixture distribution
Draw which regime you're in first, then draw the value from that regime's curve. Used for inflation's normal-vs-high decades.
Latent factor (Z)
A single shared random draw that several inputs load on, making correlated risks arrive together. The model's “kind of decade” dial — §3.7.
P50 (median)
The middle outcome: half of simulated futures land better, half worse.
P80 / P90
The value the outcome beats 80% (90%) of the time. The conservative planning figure — the build budget is funded at the P80 cost.
Standard error
How much a simulated percentage would wobble on a different random seed: √(p(1−p)/n). About ±0.11 points here — negligible.
Seed
The random generator's starting value. Locked and published (20260629), so every re-run reproduces the exact figures — no lucky-run fishing possible.
Discounting / present value
Money later is worth less than money now; each year's cash is divided by (1+rate)t to state everything in today's dollars. NPV = the discounted sum.
Fisher relation
Nominal rate ≈ real rate + inflation. The model discounts at 1.5% real plus each run's own drawn inflation (~4% at base), so high-inflation futures are discounted consistently.
Payback (discounted)
The year the discounted unrecovered balance reaches zero. “Conditional” timing counts only recovering runs; “unconditional” counts every run — §2.8.
DSCR
Debt-service coverage ratio: annual operating cash ÷ annual debt payment. Covenants test the worst single year (“min-year”) and typically want ≥1.2×.
ARPU
Average revenue per user — here the wholesale rate billed to resellers per connected customer: $480/yr at the proposed $40/mo.
Churn
The share of customers leaving per year. Modeled as a bounded hazard that front-loads and plateaus rather than compounding forever.
Price elasticity
How much demand falls when price rises. Broadband in a low-substitute rural market is necessity-grade (~−0.3), supporting the model's 2.5% haircut at the $40 step.
95th-percentile billing
Usage sampled every 5 minutes all month; the busiest 5% of readings are free; the bill is the highest sustained rate remaining × the per-megabit price. How the District bills RSPs for bandwidth.
Capex / opex
Capital expenditure (the build: hardware, labor, contingency) vs operating expenditure (crew, sites, backhaul, admin — the recurring cost to serve).
Contingency (AACE Class 4)
The overrun allowance a planning-grade estimate requires. AACE 18R-97 puts a Class-4 (concept/feasibility) estimate's accuracy at roughly −10%/+40% — exactly the range drawn here.
BOM
Bill of materials — the vendor's itemized hardware quote. This one carries $0 for tower-side labor, which is why labor is modeled explicitly.
Counterfactual
The comparison world: what households would pay with no public option. Modeled as an escalating wholesale rate — deliberately milder than retail satellite pricing.
Pro forma
A conventional single-spreadsheet projection: one best-guess per input, one confident answer out. Tidy, and structurally unable to say how likely it is to be wrong.
GAO / FTA / WSDOT CEVP
Federal and state bodies whose cost-estimating guidance requires probabilistic (Monte Carlo) risk analysis on capital projects — the practice this model follows.
Ring-fenced
Legally separated books. RCW 54.16.330 requires the telecom segment to be self-supporting — electric ratepayers cannot subsidize it, and none of this model leans on them.
RSP / reseller
Retail service provider — the local businesses that sell internet service to households over the PUD-owned network, paying the District wholesale rates. The PUD owns and operates the network; RSPs bill, support, and install.
